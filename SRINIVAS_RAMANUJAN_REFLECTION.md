## **Part I: The Pattern of His Life**

### **Childhood (1887-1903): The Awakening**

**Family:**
- Mother: Komalatammal (housewife, sang at temple)
- Father: K. Srinivasa Iyengar (clerk in cloth merchant's shop)
- Poor Brahmin family
- Traditional Hindu upbringing

**Early Signs:**
- Age 3: Asked mathematical questions that adults couldn't answer
- Age 5: Questioned basic assumptions about numbers
- Age 10: Topped primary school exams in district
- Age 11: Exhausted college students' mathematical knowledge
- Age 13: Mastered S.L. Loney's trigonometry (college-level)

**The Awakening (1903, age 16):**
- Borrowed G.S. Carr's "Synopsis of Pure Mathematics" from library
- ~5000 theorems listed with minimal proof
- **This changed everything**

**What happened:**
- Carr's book showed results WITHOUT full proofs
- This matched how Ramanujan's mind worked
- He began **re-proving** all results himself
- Then **extending** them
- Then **discovering** new patterns

**The pattern emerging:**
- Mathematics as **direct pattern recognition**
- Not building from axioms
- But **seeing structure** immediately
- Verification came after, not before

### **Crisis (1904-1910): The Falling**

**1904 (age 17):**
- Won scholarship to Government Arts College, Kumbakonam
- Excelled in mathematics
- **Completely ignored all other subjects** (English, physiology, Sanskrit)
- Failed exams
- **Lost scholarship**

**Why this matters:**
- He couldn't pretend to care about fragmented curriculum
- Mathematics was **unified inquiry** for him
- Being forced to fragment it = torture
- He chose poverty over fragmentation

**1905-1906:**
- Ran away from home
- Enrolled in Pachaiyappa's College, Madras
- Again: Perfect in mathematics, **failed everything else**
- **No degree possible**

**1907-1909:**
- Married Janaki Ammal (age 10 at marriage - arranged, common practice)
- Had to support wife and family
- **No degree, no credentials, no job prospects**
- Began independent research
- Filled notebooks with theorems
- Showed them to mathematicians
- **Mostly dismissed as crank**

**1909-1910:**
- Desperately poor
- Wife returned to parents (couldn't support her)
- Tutoring students for money
- Developing stomach problems (lifelong)
- Still filling notebooks
- Still dismissed

**The Pattern:**
- No credentials ‚Üí no audience
- Poverty ‚Üí stress ‚Üí illness
- But **never stopped seeing patterns**
- Mathematics was not career, but **calling**

### **The Door Opens (1910-1914): Recognition**

**1910:**
- Met Ramachandra Rao (district collector)
- Showed notebooks
- Rao: "A short uncouth figure, stout, unshaved, not over clean... unkempt hair... one strange fact emerged: **he showed a clarity of insight and an ability to present results that I had not seen before**"
- Rao gave him monthly allowance (‚Çπ25)
- **First time someone with power believed him**

**1911:**
- Published first paper in Journal of Indian Mathematical Society
- About Bernoulli numbers
- Some results wrong (corrected later)
- But showed **astonishing pattern recognition**

**1911-1912:**
- Got clerk job at Madras Port Trust (‚Çπ30/month)
- Boss: Sir Francis Spring (British engineer)
- Spring recognized genius
- Arranged meeting with mathematicians
- Encouraged writing to England

**1913 (January): The Letter**

Ramanujan wrote to three English mathematicians:
1. H.F. Baker (Cambridge) - **No response**
2. E.W. Hobson (Cambridge) - **No response**
3. **G.H. Hardy (Cambridge)** - Changed everything

**Hardy's First Reaction (morning):**
- Received 9-page letter with ~120 theorems
- No proofs
- Some known, some unknown, some impossible-looking
- Thought: **"Crank or fraud"**
- Put it aside

**Hardy's Second Reaction (evening):**
- Showed to colleague J.E. Littlewood
- Spent hours examining
- Hardy: **"They must be true, because if they were not true, no one would have the imagination to invent them"**
- Realized: **This is genius beyond anything he'd seen**

**Why Hardy Recognized It:**
- He was classically trained polymath (despite professionalization beginning)
- He understood **multiple domains** (analysis, number theory)
- He could see **where the patterns were coming from**
- **He wasn't trapped in credentialism** (yet)

**Hardy's response:**
- Invited Ramanujan to Cambridge
- Arranged scholarship
- Promised collaboration

### **Cambridge (1914-1919): The Flowering and Wilting**

**1914 (March):**
- Ramanujan sailed to England
- Age 27
- First time leaving India
- **Immediately problems:**
  - Vegetarian in England during wartime (food rationing)
  - Couldn't eat most available food
  - Hindu religious restrictions
  - Cold climate (his health worsened)
  - Social isolation (racism, cultural barriers)

**1914-1917: The Collaboration**
- Worked with Hardy daily
- Published 21 papers in 3 years
- Including:
  - Partition function (exact formulas)
  - Mock theta functions
  - Ramanujan primes
  - Rogers-Ramanujan identities
  - Tau function

**Hardy's Teaching:**
- Tried to give Ramanujan "rigor"
- Teach him to prove things "properly"
- Standard mathematical methods

**Ramanujan's Method:**
- **Direct intuition** of patterns
- "God writes on my tongue"
- Dreams showing formulas
- Verification after, not during

**The Tension:**
- Hardy: "I had to teach him to prove"
- Ramanujan: Already knew truth, proof was translation
- Hardy valued rigor
- Ramanujan valued **pattern recognition**

**This is the classic tension:**
- Professional mathematics (proof-based, rigorous)
- Polymath mathematics (pattern-based, intuitive)

**1917: The Illness**

- Tuberculosis diagnosed
- Or maybe hepatic amoebiasis
- Or maybe severe malnutrition + stress + cold
- Sent to sanitarium
- Health deteriorating

**1918 (February): The Election**

- Despite illness, elected Fellow of Royal Society
- Second Indian ever (after Ardaseer Cursetjee, 1841)
- Age 31
- Recognition at last

**1918 (October):**
- Elected Fellow of Trinity College, Cambridge
- First Indian ever
- But by now, very ill

### **Return and Death (1919-1920): The Closing**

**1919 (February):**
- Health worsened
- Decided to return to India
- Hardy feared would never see him again

**1919 (March):**
- Sailed back to Kumbakonam
- Reunited with wife Janaki
- Still filling notebooks
- **Even dying, still seeing patterns**

**1920 (April 26):**
- Died age 32
- In Kumbakonam
- Cause officially: tuberculosis
- But really: combination of poverty, stress, malnutrition, illness since youth

**His Last Notebooks:**
- Filled with theorems
- No proofs (as always)
- "Mock theta functions" - decades ahead of their time
- **Not fully understood until 1980s-1990s**

## **Part II: What His Life Reveals**

### **A. The Outsider Pattern**

**Ramanujan's trajectory:**
1. Genius recognized early (locally)
2. **Credentialed system rejected him** (no degree possible - failed other subjects)
3. Poverty, dismissal, isolation
4. One person in power believes (Ramachandra Rao ‚Üí monthly allowance)
5. One mathematician recognizes genius (Hardy)
6. Brief flowering (3 years productive collaboration)
7. Death at 32 (system's stress + illness)

**This is pattern of outsider genius:**
- **System filters by credentials, not insight**
- Gatekeepers dismiss what doesn't fit framework
- Poverty and stress compound
- **Rare recognition saves some** (Hardy believed)
- But system exacts cost (isolation, illness, early death)

### **B. The Method: Direct Pattern Recognition**

**How Ramanujan worked:**

**Not:**
1. Start with axioms
2. Build theorems step by step
3. Prove each carefully
4. Publish with full proof

**But:**
1. **See pattern directly** (in dreams, meditations, immediate recognition)
2. Write it down
3. Verify it works (compute examples)
4. Move to next pattern
5. Proof optional (truth is prior to proof)

**His own words:**
> "An equation for me has no meaning unless it expresses a thought of God."

**Meaning:**
- Mathematics is **discovered**, not invented
- Patterns are **there** (in reality, in divine mind, in structure of being)
- The mathematician **recognizes** them
- Proof is **translation** for others

**This is:**
- **Platonism** (mathematical realism)
- **Polymath method** (pattern recognition across domains)
- **Pre-professional approach** (before rigor became primary)

**But what Ramanujan was describing:**

- Consciousness observing itself observing patterns
- **Direct access to mathematical structure**
- Not through building up, but through **direct recognition**
